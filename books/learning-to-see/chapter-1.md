# Chapter 1 — The Room


The university room looked open, almost democratic. Rows of full-size tables. Good light. No music loud enough to distract you. People drifting in and out between classes, between nights, between lives. It felt public, neutral—like skill would sort itself out naturally.

It doesn’t.

The order wasn’t written anywhere. No rankings. No brackets. You learned it by watching who waited, who didn’t, who paid, who collected, and who never seemed to play unless there was something at stake.

What surprised Paul early on wasn’t who would play—but who wouldn’t.

Some players didn’t play strangers at all. Not for money. Not evencheap. Not for practice. They played only with people they already knew, often the same small group but sometimes larger. Most of them didn't seem to care about pool, just somewhere to hang out. Some though were regular and there almost every night. It wasn’t about protecting anything. They simply weren’t open to the room.

You could walk among them and never be part of their world. Others though would come arond eventually and let you in but not until they determined that you were safe.

None of that mattered, though.

Paul needed to get better on these tables if he was ever going to get his money back.
he needed practice.

So he stayed.

As weeks passed, he adjusted to the tables and with the consisten play,he improved. He learned the distances. The speed. How unforgiving the tables were when you were careless. He worked on making balls cleanly and consistently. Long shots stopped feeling long. Patterns began to appear where before there had only been clutter.

He started to win.

Not against the best players. Not even close. But against the ones who played too fast, or gambled too loose, or didn’t know when to stop. The worst gamblers. The worst players. sometimes for money, other times just for practice. He played anyone who would. He played cheap games when he could. Nothing like the dollar a point rotation. That had been a trap. He understood now and kept things cheap and simple.

He paid Cole back and started to put a few bucks in his pocket. He played him every chance he had.  He didn't play him for money though. He had learned his lesson.

He started to learn how gambling really worked. Who chased losses and who didn’t. Who talked themselves into bad games. Who believed confidence was the same thing as control. He wasn’t dangerous, but he wasn’t invisible anymore either.

The room started to make sense.

Some players stood out immediately.

Calder was one of them.

He was a big guy and wore jeans and cowboy boots, every time Paul saw him. He stood high on the ball, almost leaning back, feet close together like he wasn’t planning to move. He didn’t rush. Didn’t explain. Didn’t seem to care who was watching. When he played, the table felt smaller, like it belonged to him. The only time he ever seemed to tighten up was when his hot wife came in to see him. All the guys that noticed her did. She was sharp and clearly in control and she didn't seem to like it when he played pool... so he didn't play much but when he did, watch out.

He didn’t gamble much. And it was never sloppy.

Others were harder to read.

Eli was always around.

He played well—very well—and he almost always played with Iris. Not exclusively, but close to it. They practiced together, ran racks together, and seemed content to keep things that way. Whatever their connection was, it wasn’t romantic. It was simply proximity.

On the table, they looked evenly matched.

Clean shots. Careful position. Long runs that made people stop and watch. Eli liked to talk—about angles, systems, why a ball went where it did. Iris talked less. She played.

Paul didn’t see much of Eli outside of that. He would play for fun if someone asked, but he avoided playing for money, even against people he should clearly beat. Including Paul. It felt like gambling just wasn’t how he measured himself.

Iris felt different.

She played with the same precision, but there was something steadier about her. Less commentary. Less drift. She finished racks the way she started them. Paul couldn’t yet tell what separated her from Eli, only that it existed.

She also stood out in the room in a way that had nothing to do with pool. There weren’t many women there, and none like her-cute, no obvious boyfriend, no group orbiting her, no performance. She belonged there on her own terms.

Paul noticed her.

At this point, Paul thought he understood the room, the tables and the game.

Hevwas making balls. He was winning some money. He could see who was good and who wasn’t. He knew who to play and who to avoid. He had recovered from Cole, and more importantly, he believed he had learned from it.
