# Chapter 3: Paul's First Tournament


Near the end of the second semester, there was a big tournament to see who would represent Purdue at the big college tournaments in the region. The Association of College Unions International put it on. The ACUI. It was a big deal for college pool. Everyone wanted to win it.

Paul signed up. He was excited but knew there were better players and knew he would have to play his best and get a little luck to make it through.

Paul moved through the early matches with a quiet efficiency that surprised even him. No scrambling. No recalculation mid-stroke. The table looked wide. The pockets generous. The noise of the room stayed where it belonged — outside the shot.

He wasn’t invincible. Just solid.

Between matches, people said the usual things. “You’re stroking it good.” “Looks easy out there.” Paul nodded, but didn’t take the bait. Compliments had a way of getting in your head.

Then he saw Calder’s name on the bracket.

The reaction was immediate and physical — a tightening in his chest, a subtle shallowing of breath. Not panic. Recognition.

Calder didn’t rush. Didn’t scan the room. Didn’t look like he needed anything from the match. He leaned against the rail like someone waiting for a late flight, not an opponent.

Paul noticed his own vision narrowing.

The table seemed smaller now. The distance between balls felt longer. Shots that had looked obvious ten minutes earlier demanded confirmation, then reassurance, then control.

Control took time.

By the third rack, Paul knew he was in trouble — not because he was missing, but because he was trying not to.

Calder never changed.

He didn’t dominate. He didn’t pressure. He just kept doing what he did while Paul quietly fought himself. When it was over, Calder offered a nod. He knew what had happened.

That was worse.

The Loser’s Bracket

Paul barely registered the walk to the next table.

Then he saw Cole.

Cole grinned. Same grin as always. Same easy posture. Same way of standing too close when he talked, like they were still kids arguing over who had dibs talking to a cute girl.

They didn’t gamble anymore. That had been an unspoken agreement. They had become good friends-in and out of the pool hall. Too much respect. Winning didn’t feel good when it cost something real.

Paul knew, with certainty this time, that he was the better player.

That knowledge didn’t help.

The first miss came early. Not a bad choice. Just a shot he’d made a hundred times. Cole didn’t react. He never did. He simply took the table the way he always had — naturally and flamboyantly. He was good and confident and his banter was hard for Paul to think through.

Paul felt the old fear arrive fully formed.

Not fear of losing.

Fear of repeating.

The more certain Paul became that the outcome was wrong, the more the table refused to agree. By the time the match ended, he felt hollowed out — not angry, not confused, just quietly defeated by something he couldn’t argue with.

Cole clapped him on the shoulder. “You’re playing better,” he said, and meant it.

Paul nodded.

That might have been the worst part.
