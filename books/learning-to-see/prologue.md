# Prologue — The Game He Didn’t Get to Play


When Paul arrived at the university, He thought he knew how to play pool.

He had grown up on a bar box. He could make balls. He understood angles. He’d won enough cheap games to believe the rest was just refinement. Bigger tables, longer shots—nothing that felt fundamental.

The university room changed that.

It was the first place he’d spent real time on full-size tables. Not just walking past them, but living there. They exposed distance, speed, and mistakes he didn’t yet have names for. Still, he thought the adjustment was mechanical. Practice. Repetition. Time.

Then he played Cole.

Cole didn’t look like the best player in the room. He stood too high, used too much wrist, talked constantly. He never seemed focused. He never seemed serious. If Paul been asked to describe him before they played, he would have said confident, maybe reckless.

Cole suggested rotation. A dollar a point. One hundred twenty-five points in the rack.

It was one game.

Cole never had any big runs.

When the shot was obvious, he took it. When it wasn’t, he moved the cue ball somewhere that felt temporary, harmless. Paul kept waiting for his turn—for the table to open, for a mistake, for the game to turn.

It didn’t.

He never made a ball.

Not one.

The rack didn’t end so much as disappear. A point here. Two there. Nothing dramatic. Just inevitable.

One hundred twenty-five dollars.

Paul didn’t have it.

He gave Cole everything in his wallet. It wasn’t close to enough, but it was all he had. There would be no more pool for a while.

Cole told Paul to pay him later. Then he moved on, already playing someone else.

What stayed with Paul wasn’t the money.

It was the realization that he had been standing at a table, cue in his hand, believing he was playing pool—

and he wasn’t even close.
