**Author:** It would be close to true but not quite, if we were robots, but we’re not. Even if we were, it would only be true in the absence of English. What is the optimal physics based movement for striking the ball with left or right English?

**AI:** Thought for 10s
If we strip away all coaching metaphors and ask the physics question, the “optimal movement” is the one that maximizes:

repeatable tip placement on the cue ball (the intended offset)

a cue velocity vector that is as parallel as possible to the desired shot line at impact

minimal unintended transverse (sideways) velocity of the tip during the last instant before impact

minimal cue-axis rotation during the last instant (no last-millisecond steering)

Everything else is aesthetics.

What “optimal” looks like in physics terms
When you hit with left/right english, you want the cue tip to deliver an impulse to the ball at an offset from center. That offset creates:

a forward impulse (translation) and

a torque (spin)

The torque is created by the moment arm of the contact point relative to the ball’s center, not by “swiping” across the surface.

So the “optimal” cue motion is:

A straight-line cue motion (locally) in the direction you want the cue ball to initially travel
But with the whole cue shifted sideways so the tip contacts the left/right point.

That’s the crucial detail:

Offset is achieved by a lateral position change

not by a lateral motion component at impact

If you add lateral motion at the moment of contact, you increase:

miscue risk (you’re approaching closer to a glancing collision + the tip can “wipe”)

unintended spin/throw variability

sensitivity to micro-timing under pressure

The human movement that best approximates that
The best approximation (for humans) is:

“Parallel shift” delivery
Your cue path is the same one you’d use for center ball.

You translate your entire stroke system sideways (stance + shoulder line + bridge position) so the cue is now parallel to the center-ball line but offset.

You still swing “through” on that new rail.

In other words: same motor program, different starting position.

This is why the best players look like they have “one stroke” that works for everything: they’re not changing the stroke, they’re changing the pose.

Why “pivot” systems exist (and when they’re “optimal”)
There’s a second class of solution people use (intentionally or accidentally):

Pivot-based english (backhand or front-hand pivot)
You aim one way and pivot the cue to the side spin offset. This can be made to work because cue-ball deflection (squirt) + the pivot geometry can partially cancel for a given shaft/bridge length.

Physics reality check:

It is equipment-dependent (shaft endmass, tip, speed)

It is speed-dependent

It is distance-dependent (swerve changes with speed and cloth)

It’s therefore not a universal “optimum,” it’s a calibrated hack

The “parallel shift” approach is closer to an equipment-agnostic optimum because it’s simply: deliver the cue along the intended launch vector with controlled offset.

What about the actual ball behavior with english?
Even with “optimal” delivery, you still must manage these unavoidable effects:

Squirt/deflection: cue ball initially goes off-line opposite the side of english.

Swerve: the spinning cue ball curves back (depends strongly on speed and cue elevation).

Throw: object ball is thrown off the pure contact line due to friction during collision (depends on cut angle, speed, spin, and whether the cue ball is sliding/rolling at contact).

So the “optimal movement” isn’t “hit it straight and it goes straight.” It’s:

deliver a clean, stable impulse at the intended offset, while choosing speed/elevation so squirt + swerve + throw are predictable.

Practical “physics-optimal” rules (that aren’t myths)

Set the offset at address and keep it fixed.
If the offset is being created late (wrist/steer), you’ve added timing sensitivity.

You’re managing friction regimes, not following a doctrine.

L ≈ C + Δ

So to get L, you must choose:

C ≈ L − Δ

Meaning: the cue must be pointed away from the squirt direction so that after squirt, the cue ball launches on the intended line.

Line of cue-ball launch (initial CB velocity vector)

short distance / firm speed / level cue → squirt dominates → larger aim compensation
