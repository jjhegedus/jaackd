Paul thought he knew how to play pool. He grew up on a bar box, understood angles, won cheap games. Then he played Cole — and never made a ball. Not one.
Learning to See follows one player's education in a university pool room and beyond, where the real lessons had nothing to do with mechanics. The players who stood apart didn't just shoot better. They saw differently — reading the table before every shot, controlling space, committing without hesitation. Learning to play at that level meant learning to see at that level first.
The book is also based on a return to former sports excellence. The author played seriously in college in the late 1980s, developed a way of seeing the table that made him genuinely good, then stepped away from the game for twenty-seven years. When he returned at 54, the intuitions were still there — but he could no longer fully articulate them. This book is the attempt to name them.
Included at the end of the book is a record of the conversations between the author and an AI assistant that shaped the ideas in the book — unfiltered exchanges where half-formed thinking was tested, pushed back on, and refined into something precise. These conversations are included as-is because AI capabilities are changing fast, and this is what working with AI looked like while relearning how to play pool.
For anyone who has ever stood at the edge of a skill they couldn't quite name — and wanted to understand what they were actually looking at.
